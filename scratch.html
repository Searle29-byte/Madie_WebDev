Why Cycling?

I grew up in Franktown, Colorado — a small town east of Denver where the Front Range looms close enough to feel personal. Getting outside was never a hobby; it was just what you did. Cycling became my sport of choice not because it's easy, but because it isn't.

Utah's roads offer some of the most beautiful — and punishing — cycling terrain in the country.

How I Train

Training for a century is as much mental as physical. Here's how I structure my year:

  1. Base Building (January – March)
    • Long slow rides, 3–4 hours at conversational pace
    • Core and strength work 3 days a week
    • Focus on form and cadence, not speed
  2. Intensity Phase (April – May)
    • Interval training: 5x5 minute VO2 max efforts
    • Hill repeats on Provo Canyon roads
    • One long ride per week scaling from 60 to 80 miles
  3. Peak & Taper (June)
    • One final 90-mile training ride
    • Reduce volume by 40%, keep some intensity
    • Sleep, nutrition, and hydration take priority
  4. Race Day (St. George Century)
    • Early start to beat desert heat
    • Fuel every 20 miles regardless of hunger
    • Ride my own race — ignore everyone else's pace
  5. Recovery & Reflection (Post-race)
    • Active recovery: easy spinning and swimming
    • Journal what worked and what to improve
    • Start planning the next goal
